What is Lung Cancer Screening?

Lung cancer screening is used to find lung cancer before it has spread.

Low-dose computed tomography (LDCT) is used for the lung cancer screening. The scan uses low amounts of radiation to get images of your lungs. It is not painful.

You could have lung cancer even if you do not have symptoms.

LDCT will be used to refer to this scan for lung cancer screening in this decision aid.

Possible Harms

False-alarms, invasive procedures, complication

Harm: False alarms

A false alarm happens when a person has a positive screening test but does not actually have lung cancer.

How many of the 1000 people who are screened will have a false alarm? (tap to view)

Harm: Invasive Procedures

An example of an invasive procedure is a biopsy (a tiny piece of lung tissue is removed to test for cancer).

How many of the 1000 people who are screened will have an invasive procedure? (tap to view)

Harm: Complications

Invasive procedures for lung cancer testing can have complications. These complications can include bleeding in the lung, a collapsed lung, or an infection.

Harm: Exposure to Radiation

LDCT scans expose a person to radiation, which can increase his or her risk of developing cancer. We do not know how radiation from LDCT scan may affect a person's health.

Radiation exposure (tap to view)

Harm: Overdiagnosis

Lung cancer screening may find lung cancer that would not have ever caused symptoms or harmed you in your lifetime. This is called overdiagnosis.

Overdiagnosis means that some people may get treatment that they do not need.

Something else to consider about lung cancer screening

Lung cancer screening may find other health
issues that are not cancer.

Screening can find health problems like heart disease, kidney problems, and thickened lung tissue.
